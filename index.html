Health Passport Guidance

Health Passport Guidance

The Health Passport is designed for staff who have a long-term condition which hinders their performance or attendance at work. This includes non-visible and undiagnosed conditions that have a substantial and long-term impact on the employee and may be classed as a disability.

According to the Equality Act 2010, a disability is a condition that affects you physically or mentally and has a "substantial" and "long-term" negative effect on daily activities. Employers have a legal duty to offer reasonable adjustments to any employee who has a disability as defined in the Act.

  • ‚Äòsubstantial‚Äô is more than minor or trivial, e.g., it takes much longer than it usually would to complete a daily task like getting dressed.
  • ‚Äòlong-term‚Äô means 12 months or more, e.g., a breathing condition that develops because of a lung infection.
  • A progressive condition is one that gets worse over time. People with progressive conditions can be classed as disabled.
  • Automatic qualification: A person automatically meets the disability definition under the Equality Act 2010 from the day they are diagnosed with HIV infection, cancer, or multiple sclerosis.

The Health Passport is intended to help employees access the support they may need and support them to manage their health in work. They should be encouraged and supported to communicate their condition upon joining the organisation, when they change role, department, or move to another NHS employer. The passport can remain with an employee throughout their NHS career.

Where to find it

The form can be accessed on the Intranet under Reasonable Adjustments Toolkit.

How to use it

For new staff, the Health Passport can be discussed during their onboarding and induction. It is important that the passport is used positively, and the individual understands that its purpose is to support them at work. The information shared should be handled respectfully and with compassion. It can also be used as a tool to have ongoing conversations around an individual‚Äôs needs in the workplace.

When scheduling a meeting with new or existing staff to discuss workplace support you should:

  • Assure the individual that the meeting and anything included in the passport will be held confidentially.
  • Make it clear that the focus is on supporting the individual to thrive at work.
  • Make it clear that any actions you take as a manager will be recorded and timeframes agreed.
  • Ask the individual if any adjustments have been agreed with Occupational Health and if not, ask if they would like to be referred for an assessment.
  • Encourage the individual to share their thoughts throughout the meeting.
  • Ensure any actions agreed with your staff member are reasonable for the organisation, team and department.

Suggested ways to open a conversation with an employee

  • ‚ÄúI would really like to use this time today to understand more about you and how I can support you at work.‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúCan you explain what helps you thrive at work?‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúI would like to discuss the information in your Health Passport so we can look at providing the right equipment and space for you to excel in your role.‚Äù

Suggested ways to open a conversation with a line manager

  • ‚ÄúI would really like to request some time with you to discuss my Health Passport.‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúI feel I need some support in work, and these are the areas I want to discuss‚Ä¶‚Äù

If you need any further support or advice prior to meeting with your employee or manager, please contact your HR Advisor or HR Business Partner.

How to store this record

Our systems will allow documents to be held securely. It is the responsibility of the individual to look after their own personal copy of the Health Passport.

How to update it

The Passport can be used as a live document and can be revisited as the condition or needs of the individual change. Any changes should be recorded by the individual and their line manager in the space provided on the form.

Confidentiality

Any information provided by an individual is treated as sensitive data and must be kept securely. We advise that you refer to our privacy/confidentiality policy.

Please note: It‚Äôs crucial the employee gives their consent for the information to be shared wider than their immediate line manager.

Quick Reference Guide: Supporting Neurodivergent Staff with Reasonable Adjustments

What is Neurodiversity?

Neurodiversity refers to natural variations in how people think, process information, and experience the world. It includes, for example, Autism, ADHD, Dyslexia, Dyspraxia, Tourette's, and sensory processing differences. A formal diagnosis is not required to request or benefit from adjustments.

Legal Duty

Under the Equality Act 2010, neurodivergent staff may be considered disabled. Employers must consider and implement reasonable adjustments to remove or reduce workplace barriers.

When to Use the Neurodiversity Adjustments Tool

  • A staff member discloses neurodivergence
  • New joiner has declared a need for support
  • Health Passport or Wellbeing Conversation highlights support needs
  • Trigger event (e.g. role change, stress, complaints about environment)
  • Performance concerns are raised possibly related to neurodivergence

Common Workplace Challenges and Example Adjustments

Sensory overload
Noise-cancelling headphones, quiet zones, low lighting
Difficulty focusing
Structured routines, clear priorities, short breaks
Communication differences
Written over verbal communication, meeting agendas in advance
Memory / processing speed
Checklists, step-by-step instructions, visual task aids
Social or emotional fatigue
Flexible hours, reduced social interactions, buddy systems
Need for predictability
Advance notice of changes, visual timetables
Executive dysfunction
Task chunking, regular check-ins, time management tools

How to Approach the Conversation

Use empathy and openness, don't make judgements or try to pre-empt an outcome. Suggested openers could include:

  • "I want to make sure you have the right support to thrive at work. Are there any adjustments that would help?"
  • "We have a Reasonable Adjustments Toolkit to support staff who may be facing barriers in work, would it be helpful to take a look at it together?"
  • "You don't need a formal diagnosis to talk about what could help you do your best work."

Ensure privacy, maintain confidentiality, be respectful and focus on strengths. Document any adjustments identified.

What to Do Next

  • Use the Neurodiversity Adjustments Tool (in the Reasonable Adjustments Toolkit)
  • Agree and document adjustments
  • Complete the Health Passport during discussion and allow the employee to keep this for their own future use
  • Complete a Reasonable Adjustments Plan (RAP) to formally record agreement

Refer to support if needed:

  • HR Advisor / HRBP
  • Occupational Health
  • Health & Safety
  • Access to Work (for higher-cost adjustments, guidance is available in the Reasonable Adjustments Toolkit)

Review adjustments at least annually or when needed.

Reasonable Adjustments at Work

Here‚Äôs a modular overview of frequently asked questions about Reasonable Adjustments‚Äîdesigned to support inclusion, wellbeing, and contributor clarity.

üß† What are Reasonable Adjustments?

Reasonable Adjustments are changes we can make to help someone with a disability or health condition work safely and comfortably. These changes help create equity and provide an inclusive workplace for all.

üôã Who can request Reasonable Adjustments?

Anyone with a long-term health condition, disability (including non-visible or undiagnosed ones), mental health condition, or neurodiverse needs. You don‚Äôt need a formal diagnosis to start a conversation.

üõ†Ô∏è What are examples of Reasonable Adjustments?

  • Specialist equipment (e.g. ergonomic chair, software)
  • Changes to duties or workload
  • Time off for treatment or recovery
  • Support during meetings or training
  • Flexible hours or homeworking

üì£ How do I request an adjustment?

  • Speak to your Line Manager first, request a meeting with them to discuss it
  • Take advice from HR or Occupational Health if needed.
  • To support you, you can use:
    • The Neurodiversity Reasonable Adjustments Support Tool
    • The Health Passport
    • Seek advice from Occupational Health
    • Request an Access to Work assessment if needed

üß© What is a Neurodiversity Reasonable Adjustments Support Tool?

This is a questionnaire that asks you to reflect on the challenges you are facing and the type of adjustment or support that may help alleviate the problem and provide equity for you. This is for you to use as you wish, there is no need to share this with your manager unless you want to. It may also be helpful to go through this together with your manager or a colleague if you feel comfortable with that. It helps you to prepare your thoughts for the discussion about Reasonable Adjustments.

üìÑ What is the Health Passport, when and how do I use it?

The Health Passport is a simple document where you and your manager can discuss and record any support or adjustments you need at work. You can keep this document with you and use it again in future in case you change department or manager. You can find it on the Intranet under HR/Forms.

When to use it:

  • When joining or starting a new role
  • After a health change
  • Following a well-being or return-to-work conversation
  • Anytime

How to use it :

  • Ask your manager for a meeting to discuss Reasonable Adjustments
  • Refer to the Health Passport and use it to guide the conversation
  • Record what you think is relevant in the sections provided
  • Note what adjustments will be helpful and discuss this with your manager.
  • Ensure the Health Passport is kept up to date for your future reference

üí∞ What if adjustments cost money?

Your department usually covers small costs. If the adjustment is expensive, Access to Work may be able to help.

üöÄ What is Access to Work and how does it help?

It‚Äôs a government scheme that can help fund adjustments if internal options aren‚Äôt enough. You apply directly (we can help), and they may offer:

  • Assessments
  • Equipment
  • Support workers
  • Travel help

You can refer to the Access to Work Flowchart in the Reasonable Adjustments Toolkit which you can find under HR/Reasonable Adjustments Toolkit

üîí Will my health information be kept private?

Yes. Your Health Passport and any health details are treated confidentially. They‚Äôre only shared with others if you agree.

üìã Do I need medical evidence?

No, you can request Reasonable Adjustments without medical evidence. However Occupational Health advice can sometimes help us understand your needs better if that‚Äôs considered necessary.

‚úÖ What happens after adjustments are agreed?

You and your manager will:

  • Complete a Reasonable Adjustments Plan (RAP)
  • Update your Health Passport
  • Arrange the agreed adjustments
  • Review every 12 months (or sooner if needed)

You can refer to the Reasonable Adjustments Flowchart for the full process which is available in HR/Reasonable Adjustments Toolkit

üó£Ô∏è What if I don't feel supported?

If you feel your needs aren‚Äôt being met:

  • Speak to HR for advice
  • Ask for a referral to Staff Support or Occupational Health advice
  • Ask for a Workplace Risk Assessment through the Health and Safety Department

Your wellbeing matters‚Äîplease speak up.

Fatigue Risk Tool Instructions

Using the Fatigue Risk Assessment Tool

1. Introduction

This tool helps assess the level of fatigue a staff member may experience in their role. It automatically calculates a risk index based on input scores across five categories: Shift Length & Pattern, Workload & Intensity, Physical Environment, Psychological Load, and Personal Health. You can reuse the tool by clearing and re-entering scores.

2. Risk Group Column

Do not input data here. This column displays the risk group for each category automatically.

3. Category Column

This lists the individual factors you'll be scoring. Each row represents a specific aspect of the working environment or personal health. A good understanding of these elements is helpful‚Äîespecially if the staff member is willing to share.

4. Rating Criteria Column

Use this to determine the risk rating: 0 = Low, 1 = Moderate, 2 = High. For example, shift length under 10 hrs = 0, 10‚Äì12 hrs = 1, over 12 hrs = 2.

5. Input Score Column

Enter the relevant score (0, 1, or 2) based on your assessment. Do not enter anything in the total or risk index rows‚Äîthese are auto-calculated.

6. Category Total Column

Do not input data here. This column automatically calculates the total score for each risk group.

7. Max Score Column

Do not input data here. This shows the maximum possible score for each category and is used in the overall risk calculation.

8. Risk Index Row

Do not edit this row. It displays the total score and highlights the overall risk level: Low, Moderate, High, or Critical.
